## A bench between two centuries

The smell arrives before anything else — a low, mineral sharpness, somewhere between singed beeswax and hot copper. Inside the compound, Yemi Awosile's studio occupies two rooms that have not changed much since her father worked them.

On one wall: wax models in various stages of completion. On another: a propane torch, a crucible stand, a rubber vulcanizer, and a centrifugal casting machine that has been in the family since 1987.

> The wax is where I think. The metal is where I commit.

What follows is an account of how a single piece moves from concept to completed object in this studio.

## Six stages of lost-wax casting

### 01. Model Fabrication
The original model is sculpted from jeweler's wax, carved by hand or built up carefully to account for shrinkage and flow.

### 02. Spruing
Thin wax rods are attached to create channels through which metal will enter the mold.

### 03. Investing
The wax assembly is placed inside a steel flask and surrounded with investment, a plaster-silica compound.

### 04. Burnout
The flask is heated gradually until the wax vaporizes, leaving a precise negative cavity.

### 05. Casting
18-karat gold is melted, then forced into the cavity using centrifugal force.

### 06. Finishing
Once cooled, the raw casting is cleaned, cut, sanded, polished, and prepared for setting.

## Tools and systems

Awosile's studio operates on a hybrid logic: historical methods sustained by selective investment in machines that keep production consistent.

- Centrifugal casting machine
- Rubber vulcanizer
- Burnout kiln
- Flex shaft / rotary tool
- Rolling mill
- Wax 3D printer

## Business and operations

The studio operates entirely as a direct-to-client and commission business. Commission work constitutes most of the revenue, with the remainder coming from a small annual collection.

## Philosophy and reflection

The tools change. The material is the same. The metal does not know what made the wax.
